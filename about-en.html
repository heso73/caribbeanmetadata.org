About the CMS

What is the Caribbean Metadata Standard?

The first unified framework for classifying, describing and indexing Caribbean audiovisual content — a cultural and technological infrastructure.

Definition

A cultural and technological standard

The Caribbean Metadata Standard (CMS) is a structured taxonomic framework composed of six metadata families. It was designed to describe the unique identity of Caribbean audiovisual content with precision, consistency, and authenticity.

It is intended for creators, distribution platforms, cultural institutions, and researchers who wish to index, archive, or distribute Caribbean content on a global scale.

Why it is necessary

Caribbean content is invisible on global platforms

Recommendation algorithms determine what audiences see and value. Without adapted metadata, Caribbean creators are structurally disadvantaged.

A

Misclassification

Platforms have no categories for Caribbean genres, Creole dialects, and the cultural nuances specific to the region.

B

Algorithmic blindness

Recommendation systems cannot promote what they cannot interpret. What is not classified is not recommended.

C

Cultural dilution

Without precise metadata, Caribbean content is assigned to generic categories that erase its identity and reduce its economic impact.

Founding principles

The values that guide the CMS

01

Authenticity

Every tag reflects a verifiable cultural reality. The CMS does not simplify — it reveals the complexity and richness of Caribbean identities as they actually exist.

02

Rigour

The standard is built with the same exigence as existing international standards — Dublin Core, TEI, VRA Core — with which it is designed to be interoperable.

03

Accessibility

The CMS is designed to be usable by independent creators as well as large institutions. Its documentation is public, clear, and multilingual.

04

Cultural sovereignty

The CMS is developed by and for the Caribbean. It affirms the right of Caribbean creators to define their own categories, rather than submitting to those imposed by external systems.
